CRM plumbing that moves revenue

The unglamorous joins, webhooks, and idempotency keys that decide whether your pipeline numbers are real — and how to keep finance from quietly rebuilding them in a spreadsheet.

crm revenue

Every CRM eventually grows a shadow: a spreadsheet someone in finance maintains because they don't trust the dashboard. That spreadsheet is a bug report. It tells you exactly where your plumbing leaks.

The pipeline number on the executive dashboard travels through more systems than anyone admits. A form fill hits a landing page, fires a webhook, lands in a queue, gets de-duplicated against an existing contact, attributed to a campaign, and only then becomes a row that rolls up into "revenue influenced." Each hop is a chance to double-count, drop, or silently mis-attribute. The dashboard looks confident anyway.

The three leaks #

In practice, almost every "the numbers are wrong" escalation traces back to one of three places. Knowing them turns a week of forensic SQL into an afternoon.

  • Non-idempotent writes — a retried webhook creates a second deal.
  • Attribution races — the contact is created before the campaign touch is recorded, so first-touch is blank.
  • Soft-delete blindness — merged duplicates still sum into reports.

Idempotency is a revenue feature #

Treat every inbound event as something that will arrive twice. The fix is boring and total: a unique key on the event source, an upsert instead of an insert, and a dead-simple ledger of what you've already processed. Here's the shape of it against Postgres:

sql 
-- one row per external event, ever
INSERT INTO crm_events (event_id, contact_email, campaign, payload)
VALUES ($1, $2, $3, $4)
ON CONFLICT (event_id) DO NOTHING
RETURNING id;
-- empty result = duplicate, stop here. Idempotent by construction.

The ON CONFLICT (event_id) DO NOTHING line is the entire safety net. Pair it with a unique index and a retried delivery becomes a no-op instead of a phantom deal.

A query wider than the column #

Sometimes the honest example just doesn't fit the reading width. That's fine — the code block scrolls horizontally on its own rather than forcing the page to, and the rest of the prose stays put at 760px:

sql 
SELECT c.email, c.first_campaign, d.amount, d.closed_at, a.touch_count, a.first_touch_channel, a.last_touch_channel
FROM   contacts c
JOIN   deals d         ON d.contact_id = c.id AND d.stage = 'closed_won' AND d.deleted_at IS NULL
LEFT JOIN attribution a ON a.contact_id = c.id
WHERE  d.closed_at >= date_trunc('quarter', now()) ORDER BY d.amount DESC;

Trust the spreadsheet first #

When finance maintains a parallel number, don't fight it — reconcile against it. Treat their spreadsheet as the acceptance test for your pipeline. The day your dashboard matches their sheet to the dollar for a full quarter, you've earned the right to retire it.

The best revenue dashboard is the one finance stops checking against their own spreadsheet.

— what "done" actually looks like

A reconciliation checklist #

Run this monthly. It's ordered by how often each one is the actual culprit:

  1. De-duplicate contacts on a normalized email before anything sums.
  2. Exclude soft-deleted and merged records from every rollup.
  3. Confirm attribution touches exist before the deal's created_at.
  4. Reconcile currency at the rate on the close date, not today's.

What to watch #

A small set of guardrail metrics catches most regressions before finance does. Keep them on a board nobody has to remember to open:

MetricHealthyMeans trouble
Duplicate contact rate< 0.5%Idempotency gap upstream
Unattributed closed-won< 3%Attribution race condition
Dashboard vs. finance delta< $0Soft-delete leaking into sums
Webhook retry → new deal0Non-idempotent write path

None of this is clever. It's plumbing: unique keys, upserts, a reconciliation you actually run. But it's the difference between a revenue number you can defend in a board meeting and one you quietly apologize for. Boring, dependable plumbing is the whole job.
